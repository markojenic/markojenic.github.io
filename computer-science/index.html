Harvard - CS50X Course Assignments.

Week 0 - Starting from scratch.

Your assignment, quite simply, is to implement in Scratch, at scratch.mit.edu, any project of your choice, be it an interactive story, game, animation, or anything else, subject only to the following requirements:

  • Your project must use at least two sprites, at least one of which must not be a cat.
  • Your project must have at least three scripts total (i.e., not necessarily three per sprite).
  • Your project must use at least one conditional, at least one loop, and at least one variable.
  • Your project must use at least one custom block that you have made yourself (via Make a Block), which must take at least one input.
  • Your project should be more complex than most of those demonstrated in lecture (many of which, though instructive, were quite short) but it can be less complex than Oscartime and Ivy’s Hardest Game.

As this was my first assignment, I felt compelled to create a project purely from my own ideas, without external inspiration, and make something personally enjoyable.

My inspiration came from a mini-game in FIFA 06 on PSP, a game I played tirelessly when I was 13 years old. This mini-game, along with the 'Ronaldinho Joga Bonito' campaign videos by Nike Football, inspired me to spend all my free time practicing the kick-up skills shown.

The game required you to rotate the PSP and play it vertically, using the arrow keys at the bottom. The screen would display a randomly generated arrow, which you had to press to successfully complete a kick-up. The more successful kick-ups made, the smaller the timing window for pressing keys became, increasing the challenge.

Based on the available sprites in Scratch and their multiple costumes, I found that the dinosaur best represented the movements needed for my actions. The costume options included variations with his tail raised and resting. When alternated, these created a fluid motion of the tail kicking up and down. Thus, the name 'Ronal-dino' both a homage to a personal touch and a fitting application of the available sprites.

What's more, Scratch already had a Copacabana beach background; the only external element I imported was the music to complete the theme audibly.

Although my project demanded a much more complex script than the assignment required, it was an incredibly enjoyable task and served as a valuable introduction to thinking critically in code.
